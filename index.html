Sample problems

A few examples of the kind of problem in the collection. The numbered series opens July 1, 2026, one problem each day.

Sample 0.1

Vargaprakriti

Brahmagupta's name for the equation x² − Ny² = 1. Find the smallest positive integer x for which x² − 92y² = 1 admits a positive integer solution.

Faces of the Bhargava Cube

Bhargava's geometric reading of Gauss composition. Count the integer quadruples (a, b, c, d) in [1, 10⁴] for which a symmetric face sum is a perfect cube.

The House in Louvain

Dudeney's 1917 puzzle. Houses 1, 2, …, n line a street; one house balances the sums on either side. With 50 < n < 500, find the house number.

FAQs

A few common questions. Anything else? Send a note via the contribute page.

What is Project Brahmagupta?

An open archive of 108 problems in mathematics and computation, inspired by the works of Indian mathematicians from antiquity to the present. Some problems are classical (Brahmagupta's vargaprakriti, Madhava's series) and some are modern, written in the same spirit.

When does it launch?

July 1, 2026. From that day, one problem is unlocked each midnight India Standard Time, for 108 days.

Who is this for?

Anyone with a working knowledge of mathematics who enjoys turning ideas into code. The problems range in difficulty: some are accessible with high-school algebra, others reward graduate-level number theory or careful computation.

Can I submit a problem?

Yes. The contribute page accepts problem proposals. We are looking for problems honest to Indian mathematical material, either drawn from an Indian source, or new problems that share the spirit of the collection.

Is it open source?

Yes. The site code is MIT-licensed; the content (problem statements, prose) is licensed under CC BY 4.0. The repository lives on GitHub.

Will solutions be published?

Not on the site. In keeping with the Project Euler tradition, problems are released without solutions: you discover them by solving. A discussion forum may follow once the series is complete.
